# Circular 230 x AI - Chat Learnings Memo

Date: 2026-03-22

## Scope

This memo summarizes what we established in this session about how recent AI developments are impacting Circular 230, and how that evidence supports or challenges the contrarian thesis on the future of CPAs in tax prep, tax advice, and tax strategy.

## Core Learnings

1. **The clearest AI-related Circular 230 movement is in Treasury/IRS rulemaking, not Congress.**  
   Treasury and IRS proposed Circular 230 modernization in Dec. 2024, including an explicit technological competence expectation in proposed `§10.35`.[1][2]

2. **IRS commentary frames AI as a competence + due-diligence issue, not a substitute for practitioners.**  
   IRS National Tax Forum material states that AI cannot substitute for practitioner competence, highlights hallucination risk, and ties AI use to existing Circular 230 duties (`§10.22`, `§10.35`, `§10.37`, etc.).[3]

3. **Congressional records show AI oversight in tax administration, but limited direct Circular 230-AI linkage.**  
   We found hearings discussing IRS AI usage and modernization, and one hearing testimony explicitly mentioning both Circular 230 practitioner oversight functions and AI governance concerns, but no strong congressional record that directly states "AI changed Circular 230" as a legislative thesis.[4][5][6]

4. **Implication for CPA role split:**
   - **Prep:** More automation pressure on commodity form assembly and intake workflows.
   - **Advice and strategy:** Stronger relative value of credentialed human review, judgment, and sign-off under Circular 230 standards and accountability architecture.

## Evidence Highlights

### A) Treasury/IRS Rulemaking Signal

- IRS announcement: proposed regulations would modernize Circular 230 for current practice conditions and require practitioners to maintain technological competency.[2]
- Federal Register text: proposed `§10.35` competency includes understanding benefits and risks of relevant technology used in client service or handling confidential tax information.[1]

### B) IRS Practitioner Commentary Signal

- IRS National Tax Forum Circular 230 deck addresses AI directly:
  - AI is useful but cannot replace competent representation.
  - Practitioners must validate AI outputs and perform due diligence.
  - AI use intersects with existing standards: due diligence, competence, written advice, false opinions, and confidentiality obligations.[3]

### C) Congressional Hearing Signal

- **House Ways and Means (2025 ROI hearing):**
  - Testimony references Return Preparer Office and Office of Professional Responsibility as helping ensure Circular 230 practitioners are competent and ethical.
  - The same testimony discusses AI and automation benefits plus need for oversight, training, and challenge pathways.[4][5]
- **Senate Finance (2024 IRS budget hearing):**
  - IRS Commissioner testimony describes use of AI tools for risk identification and large partnership/corporate compliance work.[6]
- **Historical baseline (2011 preparer hearing):**
  - Hearing record reflects expansion of Circular 230 oversight themes for preparers, but predates current generative AI context.[7]

## How This Stacks Against the Contrarian POV

### What is supported

- "AI as empowerment, not replacement" is strongest in **advice** and **strategy**.
- Circular 230-era accountability logic strengthens demand for credentialed humans at high-risk decision points (position defensibility, representation, written advice, sign-off).

### What needs precision

- Avoid over-claiming that regulation protects all prep labor.  
  Better framing: regulation reinforces human accountability in risk-bearing moments, while low-complexity prep and intake tasks are increasingly automatable.

## Practical Thesis Language (distilled)

"AI compresses low-leverage tax labor and increases the value of accountable human judgment.  
Circular 230 does not preserve manual prep work by default, but it does preserve and elevate the role of credentialed professionals where legal risk, representation, written advice, and final sign-off require defensible human responsibility."
